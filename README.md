# nfs-diet-sample-tracking

## Project Abstract

Northern fur seals (laaqudax̂; Callorhinus ursinus) are top predators in the Bering Sea ecosystem and consume significant quantities of both important commercial and subsistence prey species. Prey consumption estimates are necessary to incorporate northern fur seals into ecosystem-based fisheries management and to understand the drivers of the significant population decline in northern fur seals that has occurred since the mid-1970s. Current diet data are essential for management of both prey and predators. Estimates of northern fur seal prey consumption have traditionally been based on identification of prey hard parts (fish otoliths, bones, and cephalopod beaks) recovered from fecal (scat) samples. Due to time and funding constraints associated with hard parts analyses, recent fur seal diet information is unavailable and cannot be incorporated in current fisheries management data products. DNA metabarcoding presents a more efficient approach for diet analysis and reduces the need for personnel with specialized expertise identifying diet items. Additionally, DNA metabarcoding could facilitate estimates of individual animals’ diets that are not possible with hard parts. NPRB recently funded a study where feeding trials of captive northern fur seals are being used to estimate biomass of known prey consumed by fur seals by developing correction factors for DNA metabarcoding data from scats. NOAA has invested considerable resources piloting the use of scat metabarcoding to estimate diet composition using samples collected in the wild. Here, we leverage these investments to analyze recent (2022-2025) northern fur seal diet samples to develop within-year methods of consumption estimates. These data will provide timely information that can inform fishery management decisions, such as through incorporation in multi-species stock assessment models, groundfish Risk Tables, and ecosystem models.
